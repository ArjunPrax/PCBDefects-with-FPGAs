\begin{abstract}
Deploying deep learning inference at the edge demands both high accuracy and low latency under tight power budgets. This paper presents a unified FPGA-accelerated edge-vision platform targeting two safety-critical applications: (1)~PCB defect classification using a ResNet18 model trained via transfer learning on the DeepPCB dataset, achieving 85.29\% FP32 and 84.93\% INT8 accuracy with only a 0.36\% quantization drop; and (2)~ADAS object detection using a pre-trained SSD300-VGG16 evaluated on COCO, reaching 38.5\% mAP@0.5 across four ADAS classes at the optimal confidence threshold, with INT8 quantization of all 35 Conv2d layers producing 39.0\% mAP --- essentially zero accuracy loss. Both inference pipelines share a common Verilog conv3x3 accelerator core deployed on the Xilinx ZCU104 Zynq UltraScale+ MPSoC, interfaced via AXI4-Lite control and AXI-Stream data buses. Estimated hardware acceleration of the convolution and ReLU layers on the ZCU104 PL fabric achieves a 3.3$\times$ throughput improvement over the on-chip ARM Cortex-A53 baseline, projecting real-time processing at 33~FPS. Our results demonstrate that transfer learning combined with post-training INT8 quantization and a modular FPGA accelerator constitutes an effective approach for edge AI in industrial and automotive domains.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The proliferation of deep neural networks has transformed computer vision, yet translating model accuracy into deployable edge systems remains a formidable engineering challenge. Two application domains with urgent real-time requirements are automated optical inspection (AOI) of printed circuit boards (PCBs) and advanced driver assistance systems (ADAS). PCB manufacturing generates tens of thousands of boards per day; a missed solder bridge or open track can propagate into costly field failures. ADAS applications demand robust detection of pedestrians and vehicles at latencies compatible with vehicle reaction times, yet must operate within the thermal and power envelopes of automotive-grade hardware.

General-purpose CPUs are increasingly inadequate for these workloads. A ResNet18 forward pass on the ARM Cortex-A53 embedded in the Xilinx ZCU104 is estimated at approximately 100~ms per frame---well below the 30~FPS target for production-line inspection and far too slow for real-time ADAS. FPGAs offer a compelling middle ground: they provide the data-parallelism needed for convolution throughput while consuming a fraction of the power of GPU accelerators and remaining reconfigurable in the field.

This paper makes the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A ResNet18 transfer-learning pipeline for PCB binary defect classification on the DeepPCB dataset~\cite{tang2019deeppcb}, achieving 85.29\% FP32 accuracy and only a 0.36\% drop after post-training INT8 quantization.
    \item A software baseline for ADAS object detection using SSD300-VGG16~\cite{liu2016ssd} pre-trained on COCO~\cite{lin2014coco}, demonstrating 38.5\% mAP@0.5 across the four ADAS classes (person, car, bus, truck) and characterising the precision-recall trade-off across confidence thresholds.
    \item A reusable Verilog conv3$\times$3 accelerator---comprising a line-buffer sliding-window engine, a combinational ReLU unit, and dual-port BRAM weight storage---interfaced to the ARM PS via AXI4-Lite and AXI-Stream, verified through simulation on the ZCU104.
    \item A comprehensive empirical characterisation of the two pipelines, including quantization sensitivity, threshold tuning, and latency measurements on Apple M4 as the development reference platform.
\end{enumerate}

The rest of the paper is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related} surveys related work. Section~\ref{sec:architecture} describes the overall system architecture. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} details the model training, quantization, and hardware design. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents experimental results for both tasks. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses findings and deployment considerations. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{CNN-Based Industrial Inspection}
Convolutional neural networks have become the dominant approach for automated visual inspection. Tang \emph{et al.}~\cite{tang2019deeppcb} introduced the DeepPCB dataset and demonstrated that deep CNNs can localise seven defect categories---open circuits, short circuits, mouse bites, spurious copper, missing holes, pin holes, and spurs---in PCB test images with high precision using a difference-image pipeline. Ding \emph{et al.}~\cite{ding2019cnn} proposed TDD-net, a tiny defect detection network tailored to PCBs, highlighting that model compactness is a first-order concern for deployment on factory-floor hardware with limited memory budgets. In contrast to detection-centric approaches, our work frames PCB inspection as binary image classification (defect / normal), which is well-suited to FPGA deployment because it eliminates the variable-length bounding-box output stage and reduces the hardware post-processing to a single threshold comparison.

\subsection{Transfer Learning for Domain Adaptation}
Pre-training on large-scale datasets such as ImageNet and fine-tuning on domain-specific data has proven consistently effective across industrial vision tasks. He \emph{et al.}~\cite{he2016resnet} showed that residual skip connections enable training of very deep networks while mitigating gradient vanishing; the residual formulation also makes the network more tolerant to weight perturbations, which is directly relevant to quantization robustness. ResNet18 in particular offers a favourable accuracy-versus-parameter trade-off for edge deployment, with 11.24M parameters fitting comfortably within the on-chip BRAM budget of the ZCU104. Yosinski \emph{et al.}~\cite{yosinski2014transferable} established empirically that low- and mid-level features (edges, textures, simple shapes) transfer across domains with negligible loss, while high-level semantic features are more task-specific. This motivates the two-phase strategy we adopt: freezing the backbone during initial head training prevents catastrophic forgetting of the transferred texture features that are directly useful for defect localisation, while the subsequent end-to-end fine-tuning phase allows the deeper layers to specialise to PCB surface characteristics.

\subsection{Object Detection and ADAS}
Single Shot MultiBox Detector (SSD)~\cite{liu2016ssd} introduced multi-scale default anchor boxes at multiple feature map resolutions, enabling real-time detection in a single forward pass without a separate region proposal stage. This end-to-end design makes SSD substantially more latency-friendly than two-stage detectors such as Faster R-CNN, which is critical for ADAS applications where detection latency directly affects braking distance. SSD300-VGG16 with COCO-pretrained weights achieves competitive performance on the person, car, bus, and truck classes that are most safety-critical for highway and urban driving scenarios~\cite{lin2014coco}. A key challenge with off-the-shelf COCO models in ADAS evaluation is threshold calibration: confidence scores are tuned implicitly to the full 80-class distribution, and direct application to a 4-class ADAS subset requires threshold re-optimisation, as we demonstrate in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

\subsection{Post-Training Quantization}
Jacob \emph{et al.}~\cite{jacob2018quantization} formalised symmetric and affine INT8 quantization for neural network inference, proving that calibration over as few as 100 representative inputs is sufficient to characterise activation distributions and recover near-FP32 accuracy. Symmetric quantization---mapping weight magnitudes to $[-127, 127]$ with a single scale factor $s = \max(|w|)/127$---is particularly well-suited to FPGA deployment because the zero-point is always zero, eliminating an add operation from every MAC unit. Quantization-aware training (QAT) can recover additional accuracy in challenging cases, but at the cost of rerunning the full training loop; for the balanced binary PCB dataset and the large-capacity VGG16 backbone used in SSD300, we find that PTQ achieves accuracy within noise of the FP32 baseline, making QAT unnecessary.

\subsection{FPGA-Based CNN Acceleration}
Qiu \emph{et al.}~\cite{qiu2016fpga} demonstrated a complete FPGA pipeline for VGG-type networks on a Xilinx Zynq device, establishing the viability of fixed-point convolutional engines with line-buffer sliding-window data-paths and DSP-based MAC arrays. Their work reported a 6.4$\times$ speedup over a mobile GPU at 4$\times$ lower power, motivating the PS-PL heterogeneous integration approach we follow. The Xilinx Vitis AI toolchain~\cite{xilinx2022vitis} automates quantization and deployment on Zynq UltraScale+ devices, but requires device access for synthesis and bitstream generation; our hand-written Verilog approach sacrifices some automation in exchange for full control over the micro-architecture and portability to simulation-only verification, which is important during early-stage URECA research where board access is intermittent.

\section{System Architecture}
\label{sec:architecture}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Overall Platform}

The platform targets the Xilinx ZCU104 Evaluation Board, which integrates a quad-core ARM Cortex-A53 Processing System (PS) and a Zynq UltraScale+ XCZU7EV Programmable Logic (PL) fabric on a single SoC. Both inference tasks share the same PS-PL integration layer; only the application software and the model weights differ between tasks.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.48\textwidth]{figures/architecture.png}
\caption{PS-PL system architecture on the Xilinx ZCU104. The ARM PS handles image pre-processing and post-processing; the PL fabric accelerates the conv3$\times$3 and ReLU layers.}
\label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

The processing flow is as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The ARM CPU captures an image and performs pre-processing (resize, normalise, INT8 quantize).
    \item Quantized pixel data are streamed to the PL via AXI-Stream DMA.
    \item The FPGA conv3$\times$3 engine executes the first convolutional layer using weights pre-loaded into dual-port BRAM.
    \item Intermediate results are returned to the PS via AXI-Stream.
    \item The CPU completes the forward pass (remaining layers, softmax) and applies the classification threshold.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{AXI Interface}

Two AXI channels connect the PS and PL:

\textbf{AXI4-Lite (control)}: Exposes a memory-mapped register file for start/reset control and status polling. Register map: \texttt{0x00}~Control, \texttt{0x04}~Status (done/busy), \texttt{0x08}--\texttt{0x14}~configuration parameters (image dimensions, stride, padding).

\textbf{AXI-Stream (data)}: Provides high-throughput, burst-mode pixel and result transfer, eliminating the overhead of register-based I/O for large activation tensors.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Task~1 --- PCB Defect Classification}

\subsubsection{Dataset}

We use the publicly available DeepPCB dataset~\cite{tang2019deeppcb} (GitHub: \texttt{tangsanli5201/DeepPCB}). The raw repository provides paired reference and test images for each board location together with bounding-box annotation text files; a board is labelled \emph{defect} if its annotation file is non-empty, and \emph{normal} otherwise. We implemented a preprocessing script (\texttt{prepare\_deeppcb.py}) that extracts the \texttt{*\_test.jpg} images, parses annotations, and organises them into an \texttt{ImageFolder}-compatible directory tree. The resulting split is 1{,}600 training images (800 defect / 800 normal) and 1{,}400 validation images (700 / 700), perfectly class-balanced.

\subsubsection{Model and Training}

We adopt ResNet18~\cite{he2016resnet} pre-trained on ImageNet as the backbone (11.24M parameters). All input images are resized to 96$\times$96 pixels. Training proceeds in two phases following the standard transfer-learning recipe:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Frozen backbone} (epochs 1--20): Only the final fully-connected classification head is trained; backbone weights are frozen. Learning rate: $10^{-3}$, weight decay: $10^{-4}$.
    \item \textbf{End-to-end fine-tuning} (epochs 21--50): All layers are unfrozen and trained with a reduced learning rate of $10^{-4}$.
\end{enumerate}

The loss function is cross-entropy. Data augmentation includes random horizontal and vertical flips, random rotation ($\pm$15°), and colour jitter. The best checkpoint (saved at epoch 41) achieves 85.29\% validation accuracy.

\subsubsection{Post-Training Quantization (PTQ)}
\label{sec:ptq}

We apply symmetric INT8 post-training quantization to all \texttt{Conv2d} layers:
\begin{equation}
x_q = \text{clip}\!\left(\text{round}\!\left(\frac{x}{s}\right),\,-128,\,127\right), \quad s = \frac{\max(|x|)}{127}.
\end{equation}
Activation ranges are calibrated using 50 batches of training data collected via forward-hook statistics. Quantized weights are exported to \texttt{.npy} arrays for BRAM pre-loading and \texttt{.json} metadata (scale factors). The INT8 model achieves 84.93\% accuracy, a drop of only 0.36 percentage points from FP32, as shown in Table~\ref{tab:accuracy}.

\subsubsection{Threshold Tuning}

The default classification threshold of 0.5 yields 77.4\% defect recall. Because a false negative (escaped defect) is more costly than a false alarm in AOI, we swept the threshold from 0.05 to 0.95 and identified two operating points of practical interest: threshold~=~0.20 maximises the F1 score (0.853, recall~=~87.1\%), while threshold~=~0.05 achieves 93.0\% recall at 75.3\% precision. Figure~\ref{fig:threshold} characterises the full precision-recall-F1 trade-off.

\subsection{Task~2 --- ADAS Object Detection}

\subsubsection{Dataset and Classes}

We evaluate on a 100-image ADAS-relevant subset of the COCO val2017 validation set~\cite{lin2014coco}, filtered to images containing at least one of the four safety-critical classes: \emph{person} (class~1), \emph{car} (class~3), \emph{bus} (class~6), and \emph{truck} (class~8). Ground-truth annotations are loaded directly from the COCO JSON file in $[x, y, w, h]$ format.

\subsubsection{Model}

We use SSD300-VGG16~\cite{liu2016ssd} with COCO-pretrained weights from \texttt{torchvision} (\texttt{SSD300\_VGG16\_Weights.DEFAULT}), without additional fine-tuning. The model takes 300$\times$300 RGB inputs and produces bounding boxes, class labels, and confidence scores. Only detections belonging to the four ADAS classes are retained for evaluation; all others are discarded before mAP computation to avoid score dilution from irrelevant categories.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Protocol}

Mean Average Precision at IoU threshold 0.5 (mAP@0.5) is computed using the 11-point interpolation method~\cite{everingham2010voc}. For each class $c$, the Average Precision is:
\begin{equation}
\text{AP}_c = \frac{1}{11}\sum_{r \in \{0, 0.1, \ldots, 1.0\}} \max_{\tilde{r} \geq r} p_c(\tilde{r}),
\end{equation}
where $p_c(r)$ is the precision at recall level $r$. A predicted box is a true positive if its IoU with the best-matching ground-truth box of the same class exceeds 0.5, and each ground-truth box may be matched at most once. The final mAP is averaged over the four ADAS classes:
\begin{equation}
\text{mAP@0.5} = \frac{1}{4}\sum_{c \in \{\text{person, car, bus, truck}\}} \text{AP}_c.
\end{equation}
A confidence threshold sweep from 0.10 to 0.90 (step 0.05) is performed to identify the operating point that maximises mAP@0.5. Only detections belonging to the four ADAS classes are counted; detections of the remaining 76 COCO classes are discarded before evaluation to avoid diluting the mAP with irrelevant categories.

\subsection{Shared Hardware Accelerator}

\subsubsection{Conv3$\times$3 Engine}

The Verilog module \texttt{conv3x3\_engine.v} implements a parametric INT8 convolution engine. Key design choices:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Fixed-point arithmetic}: 8-bit signed inputs and weights; 16-bit signed accumulation to prevent overflow across nine concurrent MAC operations ($9 \times (-128 \times -128) = 147{,}456 < 2^{17}$, safely within a 16-bit signed range of $\pm32{,}767$).
    \item \textbf{Line-buffer pipeline}: Three line buffers store one row of the input feature map each. As new pixels arrive over AXI-Stream, the buffers shift to maintain a 3$\times$3 sliding window, achieving pixel-by-pixel throughput after an initial fill latency of $2W$ clock cycles (where $W$ is the input width).
    \item \textbf{Weight interface}: A 72-bit flat bus (\texttt{weight\_flat[71:0]}) packs all nine 8-bit kernel coefficients combinationally from the dual-port BRAM, avoiding a sequential weight-read FSM and reducing critical-path depth.
\end{itemize}

The pipeline produces one output activation per clock cycle at steady state. For the first ResNet18 convolutional layer (64 filters, 3$\times$3 kernel, 96$\times$96 input), the estimated processing time at a 100~MHz PL clock is:

\begin{equation}
T_\text{conv} = \frac{N_\text{out} \times H \times W}{f_\text{clk}} = \frac{64 \times 96 \times 96}{100 \times 10^6} \approx 5.9\;\text{ms},
\end{equation}

where $N_\text{out}$ is the number of output channels. Summing across all ResNet18 convolutional layers and accounting for data transfer overhead, the projected end-to-end PL accelerator time of 30~ms is consistent with this per-layer estimate.

\subsubsection{ReLU Module}

\texttt{relu.v} is a purely combinational module that clips the 16-bit accumulator output to zero: $\text{out} = \max(0, \text{in})$. It adds zero pipeline stages and introduces no additional latency. Being purely combinational, the ReLU is absorbed into the conv engine's output stage and shared across both inference pipelines without any hardware duplication.

\subsubsection{AXI Wrapper}

\texttt{axi\_conv\_wrapper.v} integrates the conv engine, ReLU, and weight BRAM into a single AXI-compliant IP core. The AXI4-Lite slave decodes register writes at addresses \texttt{0x00}--\texttt{0x14} for start/reset control and image configuration; the \texttt{done} and \texttt{busy} status bits at \texttt{0x04} allow the ARM PS to poll completion without interrupts. The AXI-Stream slave accepts quantized pixel bytes from the PS DMA engine at full bus bandwidth, and the AXI-Stream master returns 16-bit accumulator values to an output buffer in PS DDR memory. The wrapper was verified for interface correctness using Icarus Verilog (\texttt{-g2012}), confirming zero port-width mismatches across all three submodule instantiations.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Quantization Robustness}

The 0.36\% accuracy drop from FP32 to INT8 on the PCB task confirms that ResNet18 is robust to post-training quantization on balanced binary datasets. The dominant source of accuracy loss in early experiments was found to be a missing \texttt{load\_state\_dict} call before Conv2d weight patching; ensuring all batch normalisation and fully connected layers carry their trained weights before quantization is essential. With this correction, the drop is negligible in practical terms.

\subsection{Threshold Strategy for AOI Deployment}

In automated optical inspection, the asymmetric cost of false negatives vs. false positives makes a fixed threshold of 0.5 suboptimal. Our sweep demonstrates that threshold~0.05 recovers 93.0\% of defective boards---a meaningful improvement over the 77.4\% achieved at 0.5---without any model change. This represents a practical engineering lever for production deployment.

\subsection{ADAS Baseline Calibration and INT8 Robustness}

The 63\% relative mAP gain from lowering the threshold to 0.10 reveals that SSD300 assigns lower confidence scores on a compact 100-image evaluation set than on the full COCO validation benchmark. The per-class ordering (person~$>$~car~$>$~bus~$>$~truck) is consistent with COCO training class frequencies. Truck's low AP (0.117) is partly attributable to its rarity in this subset and its visual similarity to bus at medium range, suggesting that a domain-adapted fine-tuning step would yield significant improvements.

INT8 post-training quantization of SSD300-VGG16 (35 Conv2d layers, symmetric per-tensor, 50-image calibration) yields a simulated mAP of 0.390 versus the FP32 baseline of 0.385 --- a $+$0.5\,pp change that is well within evaluation variance on a 100-image set. This is consistent with the well-known robustness of VGG-family backbones to weight quantization and confirms that the ADAS pipeline can be deployed in INT8 on resource-constrained hardware without sacrificing detection quality.

\subsection{Shared Accelerator Argument}

Both PCB and ADAS pipelines rely on 3$\times$3 convolutions as their primary compute primitive---the \texttt{conv3x3\_engine.v} accelerator is therefore directly reusable across tasks by re-loading weights into BRAM. This modularity is a key advantage of the proposed platform design: adding a new vision task requires only new weight files and application-layer Python code, not new RTL.

\subsection{Limitations}

The performance figures in Table~\ref{tab:performance} are projected estimates; full Vivado synthesis and on-board measurement on the ZCU104 are deferred to future work. Similarly, ADAS quantization and FPGA deployment of SSD300 are identified as the next engineering milestones. The evaluation dataset for ADAS is limited to 100 images; a larger evaluation against the full COCO 5k val set would provide statistically more robust mAP estimates.

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We have presented a unified FPGA-accelerated edge-vision platform that addresses two safety-critical inference tasks on the Xilinx ZCU104: PCB defect classification and ADAS object detection. ResNet18 fine-tuned on DeepPCB achieves 85.29\% FP32 accuracy, which is preserved to within 0.36\% under INT8 post-training quantization. SSD300-VGG16 achieves 38.5\% mAP@0.5 across four ADAS classes at the optimal confidence threshold, with a threshold-tuning procedure offering a zero-cost accuracy improvement of over 60\% in mAP relative to the default setting; INT8 post-training quantization of all 35 Conv2d layers preserves this accuracy with a simulated mAP of 39.0\%. A reusable Verilog conv3$\times$3 accelerator---verified through simulation and interfaced via AXI4-Lite and AXI-Stream---provides an estimated 3.3$\times$ throughput improvement on the ZCU104 PL over the ARM CPU baseline. These results support the conclusion that transfer learning, post-training quantization, and modular FPGA acceleration together form a practical and extensible methodology for edge AI deployment in industrial and automotive settings.
